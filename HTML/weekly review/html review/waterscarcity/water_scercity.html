Water Scarcity & Quality in Chennai

The 2019 Chennai water crisis was a water crisis occurring in India, most notably in the coastal city of Chennai in Tamil Nadu. On 19 June 2019, Chennai city officials declared that "Day Zero", or the day when almost no water is left, had been reached, as all the four main reservoirs supplying water to the city had run dry. Two years of deficient monsoon rainfall, particularly in late 2017 and throughout much of 2018 had led to this crisis. Because tap water has stopped running, some families have been relying on alternative water sources such as distant, unreliable public water pumps, and costly private water tankers.

Causes of Water Scarcity

Chennai faces severe water scarcity due to a combination of natural and human-made factors. The city depends heavily on monsoon rains, and when rainfall is delayed or insufficient, water levels in reservoirs and lakes drop drastically. Rapid urbanization and population growth have increased demand, while over-extraction of groundwater has depleted aquifers, making it difficult to meet daily needs. At the same time, pollution from industries and untreated sewage has contaminated many local water sources, reducing the availability of clean water. The destruction of lakes and wetlands for construction has further limited natural storage systems that once helped the city during dry months. Adding to this, climate change has caused irregular weather patterns, leading to droughts and extreme shortages. Poor water management practices, such as pipeline leaks and limited adoption of rainwater harvesting, have also worsened the crisis, making water scarcity one of the most pressing challenges for Chennai’s residents.
